Why now

Three hires. Two new motions. One unresolved story.

Most companies can carry a little narrative ambiguity when they're founder-led. The moment that ambiguity starts costing you is when you hand the story to other people — and you're doing that twice at once.

December 2025

First Enterprise Sales Manager.

An AE who's about to run long, multi-stakeholder evaluations in the US, Europe, and EMEA — on a story that shifts between "intelligence" and "automation" depending on which page the prospect lands on.

January 2026

First dedicated Product Manager.

Hired to turn your internal scraping stack into public APIs by year-end. A new ICP, a new motion, and a new set of expectations that will inherit whatever ambiguity already exists in the core story.

Right now

Founder-led to team-led.

You closed the million-dollar deals personally. The narrative in your head is clear. What's unclear is whether that same clarity is on the page — or whether your new AE and PM will have to reverse-engineer it from scratch.

Our read

This isn't an identity problem. It's a promise alignment problem.

Anakin knows what it is. You know what it is. The customers paying seven figures know what it is. The problem isn't that the company is confused — it's that the external story hasn't caught up with the operational shape the company has taken.

Internally, you almost certainly have an implicit map: which ICP buys which product, what's infrastructure versus what's automation, where the enterprise deal ends and the developer API begins. Externally, that map is still merged into a single homepage promise that does two jobs at once and neither as sharply as it could.

That's fixable. And it's exactly the kind of work that gets harder the longer a new AE and PM operate without it.

What the work looks like

Three moves that separate the stories without losing the company.

We're not proposing a rebrand. You've earned the ground you're on. What we'd do instead is give each motion its own clean promise — so the enterprise AE and the developer PM are selling products, not interpreting a website.

MOVE 01

Name the buyer. Name the promise. In their words.

We'd run customer and prospect interviews with enterprise pricing leaders at companies like your current customers — and separately, with the developer and platform leads who'd be the API buyer. Not to validate what you already know, but to surface the exact language each audience uses when they describe what Anakin is, and what they expect it to do.

That language becomes the top of your homepage, the first slide of your AE's deck, and the opening line of every sales call. When a buyer reads their own words back, the sale gets shorter.

MOVE 02

Reframe two promises as one company.

"Intelligence engine" and "automated pricing engine" aren't competing identities — they're two product lines that need their own clean promises. We'd build two aligned messaging frameworks under one corporate narrative: a sharper, infrastructure-grade promise for the enterprise intelligence offer, and a developer-native story for the API product that doesn't borrow credibility from the enterprise motion.

The goal: a buyer who lands on Anakin and instantly knows which product solves their problem — and a company that doesn't have to become two companies to tell both stories.

MOVE 03

Extend the narrative into sales and product surfaces.

A repositioned homepage is the visible artifact. The leverage is in what happens behind it — the sales deck your new AE walks into seven-figure evaluations with, the API positioning your PM launches against, the competitive one-pagers, the first-meeting opening. These are what the commercial team you've just hired will need as they build the motion you're handing them.

We build the narrative system so it doesn't live in one place and die there. Your team picks it up and runs it across every surface the buyer touches.
